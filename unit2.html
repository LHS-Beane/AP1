Dynamics Roadmap

1. Identify the Object

Only draw forces acting on the object you are analyzing. Do not draw forces the object exerts on other things.

2. Draw the FBD

Represent every force as an arrow starting from the object. Label force types clearly.

3. Choose Axes

Use horizontal/vertical for flat surfaces and rotated axes for ramps.

4. Apply $\Sigma F = ma$

Use Newton's second law separately in each direction.

AP Exam Mindset: Dynamics questions usually reward correct representations and reasoning before final numbers. A strong FBD is often half the solution.
2

Quick Diagnostic: Catch the Misconceptions

Question 1: Constant Velocity

A box slides to the right at constant velocity. What must be true about the net force?

Explanation

Constant velocity means acceleration is zero. Since $\Sigma F = ma$, the net force must be zero.

Question 2: Normal Force

A box rests on a horizontal table with no vertical acceleration. Which statement is true?

Explanation

The normal force is a contact force. On a level surface with no other vertical forces and no vertical acceleration, $F_N=mg$.

Question 3: Action-Reaction Pair

A student pushes a wall. The wall pushes back on the student with...

Explanation

Newton's third law pairs are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects.

3

Newton's Laws: Student-Friendly Meaning

Newton's 1st Law

If net force is zero, velocity stays constant. That includes staying at rest.

No net force does not mean no motion. It means no change in motion.

Newton's 2nd Law

The net force determines the acceleration.

$$\Sigma \vec{F}=m\vec{a}$$

Acceleration points in the direction of the net force.

Newton's 3rd Law

Forces come in interaction pairs: A on B and B on A.

Third-law forces do not cancel because they act on different objects.

Force Types You Must Recognize

Weight$$F_g=mg$$Gravity's force on an object. Direction: down toward Earth.
Normal Force$$F_N$$Contact force perpendicular to a surface. Not always equal to $mg$.
Friction$$f_k=\mu_k F_N$$Kinetic friction opposes sliding motion.
Static Friction$$f_s\leq \mu_s F_N$$Static friction adjusts up to a maximum value.
Tension$$F_T$$Pulling force from a rope or string along the rope.
Spring Force$$F_s=kx$$Restoring force from a stretched or compressed spring.
Common trap: Do not invent a “force of motion.” If an object is moving right, that does not automatically mean there is a rightward force.

Friction: Static vs. Kinetic

Friction Decision Tree

  1. Is it sliding? Use $f_k=\mu_k F_N$.
  2. Is it not sliding? Static friction adjusts as needed.
  3. Maximum static friction: $f_{s,max}=\mu_s F_N$.
  4. If needed force exceeds max static friction: it starts sliding.
Major AP trap: $f_s$ is not automatically equal to $\mu_sF_N$. That is only the maximum possible value.

Scaffolded Dynamics Problems

Level 1 • Force Identification

Problem 1: Book on a Table

A book rests on a table. It is not moving.

  1. Draw or describe the forces on the book.
  2. What is the net force?
  3. Does the book exert a force on the table?
Reveal solution

Forces on book: weight downward and normal force upward. Since acceleration is zero, $F_N=F_g$ and net force is zero. The book exerts a downward contact force on the table; the table exerts an upward normal force on the book.

Level 2 • Net Force

Problem 2: Horizontal Push

A $6.0\,\text{kg}$ cart is pushed right with $36\,\text{N}$. Friction is negligible.

  1. What is the net force?
  2. What is the acceleration?
Reveal solution

$\Sigma F=36\,\text{N}$ right. $a=\Sigma F/m=36/6=6.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ right.

Level 3 • Friction Included

Problem 3: Push with Friction

A $10\,\text{kg}$ box is pushed right with $70\,\text{N}$ across a floor. Kinetic friction is $25\,\text{N}$ left.

  1. Draw the FBD.
  2. Calculate the net force.
  3. Calculate the acceleration.
Reveal solution

Horizontal forces: applied force right and friction left. Vertical forces balance. $\Sigma F_x=70-25=45\,\text{N}$. $a=45/10=4.5\,\text{m/s}^2$ right.

Level 4 • Kinetic Friction

Problem 4: Coefficient of Friction

A $5.0\,\text{kg}$ block slides across a horizontal surface with $\mu_k=0.30$. Use $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. Find the normal force.
  2. Find the kinetic friction force.
  3. If no other horizontal force acts, what is the acceleration?
Reveal solution

$F_N=mg=(5)(10)=50\,\text{N}$. $f_k=\mu_kF_N=(0.30)(50)=15\,\text{N}$. If friction is the only horizontal force, $a=15/5=3.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ opposite the motion.

Level 5 • Static Friction

Problem 5: Will It Move?

A $20\,\text{kg}$ crate rests on the floor. The coefficient of static friction is $0.50$. A student pushes horizontally with $80\,\text{N}$.

  1. Find $f_{s,max}$.
  2. Does the crate move?
  3. What is the actual static friction force?
Reveal solution

$F_N=mg=(20)(10)=200\,\text{N}$. $f_{s,max}=\mu_sF_N=(0.50)(200)=100\,\text{N}$. Since the applied force is only $80\,\text{N}$, the crate does not move. Actual static friction is $80\,\text{N}$ opposite the push.

Level 6 • Inclined Plane

Problem 6: Frictionless Ramp

A $4.0\,\text{kg}$ block slides down a frictionless $30^\circ$ incline. Use $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. Find the component of gravity parallel to the ramp.
  2. Find the acceleration down the ramp.
  3. Find the normal force.
Reveal solution

$F_{g,\parallel}=mg\sin\theta=(4)(10)(0.5)=20\,\text{N}$. $a=F_{net}/m=20/4=5.0\,\text{m/s}^2$. $F_N=mg\cos30^\circ=(4)(10)(0.866)=34.6\,\text{N}$.

Level 7 • Connected Objects

Problem 7: Two Blocks and a Rope

A $3.0\,\text{kg}$ block on a frictionless table is connected over a pulley to a hanging $2.0\,\text{kg}$ mass. Use $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. What external force accelerates the two-block system?
  2. What is the acceleration of the system?
  3. What is the tension in the rope?
Reveal scaffold

Treat both masses together first. The hanging weight is the unbalanced external force on the system.

Reveal solution

Total mass is $5.0\,\text{kg}$. External driving force is hanging weight: $F_g=(2)(10)=20\,\text{N}$. $a=20/5=4.0\,\text{m/s}^2$. For the table block, $T=ma=(3)(4)=12\,\text{N}$.

Level 8 • Elevator / Apparent Weight

Problem 8: Scale in an Elevator

A $60\,\text{kg}$ student stands on a scale in an elevator accelerating upward at $2.0\,\text{m/s}^2$. Use $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. Draw the FBD.
  2. What does the scale read?
Reveal solution

Forces: normal force up, weight down. Upward is positive. $F_N-mg=ma$. $F_N=m(g+a)=60(10+2)=720\,\text{N}$. The scale reads $720\,\text{N}$.

AP-Style Reasoning Practice

Paragraph Response Prompt

A student says: “If an object is moving to the right, there must be a net force to the right.”

Write a paragraph that agrees or disagrees. Use Newton's laws and an example.

Reveal strong response

The statement is not always correct. Newton's second law says that net force determines acceleration, not velocity. An object moving to the right at constant velocity has zero acceleration, so the net force on it is zero. A net force to the right would mean the object is accelerating to the right, so its rightward velocity would be increasing or its leftward velocity would be decreasing.

Translation Between Representations

A cart has a constant net force to the right. It starts from rest.

  1. Describe the acceleration.
  2. Describe the velocity over time.
  3. Sketch what a velocity-time graph would look like.
Reveal strong response

The acceleration is constant and to the right. Since the cart starts from rest, its velocity increases linearly in the positive direction. A velocity-time graph would be a straight line with positive slope starting at zero.

Unit 5–6 Roadmap

5.1–5.2 Motion

Angular position, velocity, acceleration, and how they connect to linear motion using radius.

5.3–5.6 Cause of Rotation

Torque causes angular acceleration. Rotational inertia resists changes in rotational motion.

6.1–6.2 Energy

Rotating objects can have rotational kinetic energy. Torque can do work.

6.3–6.6 Momentum & Rolling

Angular momentum can be conserved. Rolling combines translation and rotation. Satellites connect gravity and circular motion.

Big AP idea: Rotation is not a brand-new course. It mirrors linear mechanics: position → velocity → acceleration, force → torque, mass → rotational inertia, momentum → angular momentum, kinetic energy → rotational kinetic energy.

Quick Diagnostic: Common Rotation Traps

Question 1: Torque

A force is applied to a door at the hinge. What torque does it produce about the hinge?

Explanation

Torque depends on force, lever arm, and angle. If the force acts at the pivot, $r=0$, so $\tau=0$.

Question 2: Rotational Inertia

Two objects have the same mass and radius. One has more mass near the edge. Which has greater rotational inertia?

Explanation

Rotational inertia depends on how mass is distributed relative to the axis. Mass farther from the axis contributes more.

Question 3: Rolling Without Slipping

For rolling without slipping, which relationship is true?

Explanation

For rolling without slipping, the center of mass speed is related to angular speed by $v=\omega r$.

Core Relationships

Arc Length$$s=r\theta$$Use radians.
Tangential Speed$$v=r\omega$$Farther from axis = faster tangential speed.
Tangential Acceleration$$a_t=r\alpha$$Due to changing angular speed.
Centripetal Acceleration$$a_c=r\omega^2=\frac{v^2}{r}$$Due to changing direction.
Trap: All points on a rigid rotating object have the same $\omega$, but points farther from the axis have larger $v$.

5.4 Rotational Inertia

What $I$ Means

Rotational inertia measures how hard it is to change an object's rotational motion.

Key idea: Mass farther from the rotation axis increases $I$ more strongly.

Common AP-level comparisons:

  • Hoop/ring: mass far from center → large $I$
  • Solid disk: mass spread inward → smaller $I$
  • Point mass: $I=mr^2$

5.5 Rotational Equilibrium

Equilibrium Conditions

For an object to be in full equilibrium:

Translational Equilibrium$$\Sigma F_x=0 \quad \text{and} \quad \Sigma F_y=0$$
Rotational Equilibrium$$\Sigma \tau=0$$
Trap: An object can have zero net force but still rotate if net torque is not zero.

5.6 Newton's Second Law in Rotational Form

Rotational Dynamics

$$\Sigma \tau=I\alpha$$

This is the rotational version of $\Sigma F=ma$.

Meaning: More net torque creates more angular acceleration. More rotational inertia creates less angular acceleration.

6.1 Rotational Kinetic Energy & 6.2 Torque and Work

Energy Equations

Rotational KE$$K_{rot}=\frac12 I\omega^2$$Energy due to spinning.
Total KE for Rolling$$K=\frac12 mv^2+\frac12 I\omega^2$$Translation plus rotation.
Rotational Work$$W=\tau\Delta\theta$$Torque acting through angular displacement.
Power$$P=\tau\omega$$Rate of rotational work.

6.3 Angular Momentum & 6.4 Conservation

Angular Momentum

Angular Momentum$$L=I\omega$$For a rigid rotating object.
Angular Impulse$$\Delta L=\tau\Delta t$$Net torque acting over time changes angular momentum.
Conservation: If external net torque is zero, total angular momentum stays constant.

Skater Pulls Arms In

If $L_i=L_f$, then:

$$I_i\omega_i=I_f\omega_f$$

Explain like AP

When the skater pulls arms inward, rotational inertia decreases. With negligible external torque, angular momentum is conserved, so angular speed increases.

6.5 Rolling

Rolling Without Slipping

Rolling without slipping connects translation and rotation:

Rolling Constraint$$v=\omega r \quad \text{and} \quad a=\alpha r$$
Big idea: A rolling object has both translational kinetic energy and rotational kinetic energy.

Objects with larger $I$ have more energy tied up in rotation, so they usually have less translational speed after rolling down the same height.

6.6 Motion of Orbiting Satellites

Orbiting Satellite Model

Gravity provides the centripetal force:

$$F_g=F_c$$

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2}=\frac{mv^2}{r}$$

$$v=\sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

Trap: The satellite is falling toward Earth, but its tangential velocity keeps it missing Earth.

Scaffolded Practice Problems

Level 1 • Rotational Kinematics

Problem 1: Angular Speed

A wheel rotates through $12\,\text{rad}$ in $3.0\,\text{s}$ at constant angular speed.

  1. Find angular speed.
  2. If radius is $0.50\,\text{m}$, find tangential speed at the rim.
Reveal solution

$\omega=\Delta\theta/\Delta t=12/3=4.0\,\text{rad/s}$. $v=r\omega=(0.50)(4.0)=2.0\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 2 • Torque

Problem 2: Door Handle

A student pushes perpendicular to a door with $35\,\text{N}$ at a distance of $0.80\,\text{m}$ from the hinge.

  1. Find the torque.
  2. How would the torque change if the force were applied at $0.40\,\text{m}$?
Reveal solution

$\tau=rF\sin90^\circ=(0.80)(35)=28\,\text{N·m}$. At $0.40\,\text{m}$, torque becomes $14\,\text{N·m}$, half as large.

Level 3 • Rotational Equilibrium

Problem 3: Balanced Beam

A $6.0\,\text{kg}$ mass hangs $0.40\,\text{m}$ left of a pivot. A $3.0\,\text{kg}$ mass hangs on the right. Where should the $3.0\,\text{kg}$ mass be placed to balance?

Reveal solution

Set clockwise torque equal to counterclockwise torque: $m_1gd_1=m_2gd_2$. Gravity cancels. $(6)(0.40)=(3)d_2$, so $d_2=0.80\,\text{m}$.

Level 4 • Rotational Dynamics

Problem 4: Pulley Angular Acceleration

A net torque of $18\,\text{N·m}$ acts on a wheel with rotational inertia $I=6.0\,\text{kg·m}^2$.

  1. Find angular acceleration.
  2. If it starts from rest, find angular speed after $4.0\,\text{s}$.
Reveal solution

$\alpha=\tau/I=18/6=3.0\,\text{rad/s}^2$. $\omega=\omega_0+\alpha t=0+(3)(4)=12\,\text{rad/s}$.

Level 5 • Rotational Energy

Problem 5: Spinning Disk

A disk has rotational inertia $I=0.80\,\text{kg·m}^2$ and spins at $10\,\text{rad/s}$.

  1. Find rotational kinetic energy.
  2. If the disk's angular speed doubles, what happens to $K_{rot}$?
Reveal solution

$K_{rot}=\frac12I\omega^2=\frac12(0.80)(10)^2=40\,\text{J}$. Doubling angular speed quadruples rotational kinetic energy.

Level 6 • Rolling

Problem 6: Rolling Disk

A solid disk rolls without slipping with mass $2.0\,\text{kg}$, radius $0.30\,\text{m}$, and center-of-mass speed $4.0\,\text{m/s}$. For a solid disk, $I=\frac12MR^2$.

  1. Find angular speed.
  2. Find translational kinetic energy.
  3. Find rotational kinetic energy.
Reveal solution

$\omega=v/r=4.0/0.30=13.3\,\text{rad/s}$. $K_{trans}=\frac12mv^2=16\,\text{J}$. Since $I=\frac12MR^2$, $K_{rot}=\frac12I\omega^2=\frac14Mv^2=8\,\text{J}$. Total KE is $24\,\text{J}$.

Level 7 • Angular Momentum

Problem 7: Pulling Arms In

A rotating student has $I_i=5.0\,\text{kg·m}^2$ and $\omega_i=2.0\,\text{rad/s}$. The student pulls their arms in so $I_f=2.0\,\text{kg·m}^2$.

  1. Find final angular speed.
  2. Explain why the speed changes.
Reveal solution

With negligible external torque, $I_i\omega_i=I_f\omega_f$. $(5)(2)=(2)\omega_f$, so $\omega_f=5.0\,\text{rad/s}$. Rotational inertia decreases, so angular speed increases to conserve angular momentum.

Level 8 • Satellite Motion

Problem 8: Orbit Reasoning

A satellite moves in a circular orbit around Earth. Its orbital radius increases.

  1. Does orbital speed increase, decrease, or stay the same?
  2. Justify using the orbit speed relationship.
Reveal solution

Orbital speed decreases. Since $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$, increasing $r$ makes the value inside the square root smaller.

AP-Style Reasoning Practice

Paragraph Prompt: Torque Misconception

A student says: “A larger force always creates a larger torque.”

Write a paragraph that agrees or disagrees. Use lever arm and angle in your explanation.

Reveal strong response

The claim is not always correct. Torque depends on the force, the distance from the pivot, and the angle between the force and lever arm. A smaller force applied farther from the pivot or more perpendicular to the lever arm can create a larger torque than a bigger force applied close to the pivot or along the lever arm. The relationship is $\tau=rF\sin\theta$.

Paragraph Prompt: Rolling Race

A hoop and a solid disk roll without slipping from rest down the same ramp. They have the same mass and radius.

Which reaches the bottom first? Explain using rotational inertia and energy.

Reveal strong response

The solid disk reaches the bottom first. Both objects lose the same gravitational potential energy, but the hoop has greater rotational inertia because more of its mass is farther from the axis. More of the hoop's energy becomes rotational kinetic energy, leaving less translational kinetic energy. Therefore the hoop has a smaller center-of-mass speed at the bottom.

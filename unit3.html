Energy Review Roadmap

1. Define the System

Decide what objects are inside the system. Energy can move within the system or be transferred across its boundary.

2. Choose Zero Height

Gravitational potential energy depends on your reference point. Motion does not.

3. Track Energy Forms

Use bar charts before equations: $K$, $U_g$, $U_s$, thermal/internal energy, and external work.

4. Use a Law

Use conservation of energy or the work-energy theorem depending on the situation.

AP Exam Mindset: The answer is rarely just a number. Students must explain why the equation applies and what the energy changes mean physically.

Quick Diagnostic: What Is the Real Weakness?

Use this as a 5-minute warmup. Students answer first, then reveal explanations.

Question 1: Reference Height

A book is on a table. A student chooses the tabletop as $h=0$. Another student chooses the floor as $h=0$. Which statement is true?

Explanation

Potential energy depends on the chosen reference point. Changes in potential energy are what matter for motion.

Question 2: Heavier vs. Lighter Objects

Two objects are dropped from the same height with negligible air resistance. Object A has twice the mass of Object B. How do their speeds compare right before impact?

Explanation

Using $mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, mass cancels. The heavier object has more energy, but it also requires more energy per speed because it has more mass.

Question 3: Friction

A block slides down a rough ramp. Compared with a frictionless ramp of the same height, the block reaches the bottom with...

Explanation

Total energy is conserved if thermal/internal energy is included, but mechanical energy decreases when friction does negative work.

3

Core Concepts Students Must Own

Energy Is a Tracking Tool

Energy helps us predict changes in motion and position without needing to analyze every force during the motion.

Energy is not “used up.” It is stored, transferred, or transformed.

System Choice Matters

If the system is the object alone, gravity may be external work. If the system is object + Earth, gravitational potential energy is stored inside the system.

Student-friendly language

Ask: “Did I include the thing causing the interaction inside my system?” If yes, it can be potential energy. If no, it may be external work.

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy usually means:

$$E_{mech}=K+U_g+U_s$$

Mechanical energy is conserved only when nonconservative work is zero or negligible.

Nonconservative Work

Friction, applied pushes, drag, and motors can change mechanical energy.

$$W_{nc}=\Delta E_{mech}$$

Equation Toolbox: Meaning Before Plugging In

Kinetic Energy$$K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$$Energy due to motion. Speed is squared.
Gravitational PE$$U_g=mgh$$Energy stored by object-Earth position.
Spring PE$$U_s=\frac{1}{2}kx^2$$Energy stored in a stretched/compressed spring.
Work$$W=Fd\cos\theta$$Energy transferred by a force over a displacement.
Work-Energy$$W_{net}=\Delta K$$Net work changes kinetic energy.
Power$$P=\frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}=Fv$$Rate of energy transfer.
Common AP trap: $W=Fd$ only works when force is constant and in the same direction as displacement. Otherwise use $W=Fd\cos\theta$ or area under an $F$ vs. $x$ graph.

Scaffolded Practice: Concept → Math → AP Reasoning

Level 1 • Energy Identification

Problem 1: Dropped Ball

A $2.0\,\text{kg}$ ball is held $5.0\,\text{m}$ above the ground and released from rest. Use $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. What is the system?
  2. What energy does the system have at the top?
  3. What energy does it have right before impact?
Reveal scaffold

Use ball + Earth as the system. At the top: $U_g$. At the bottom: mostly $K$, assuming negligible air resistance.

Reveal solution

$U_g=mgh=(2)(10)(5)=100\,\text{J}$. At the bottom, $K=100\,\text{J}$.

Level 2 • Reference Point

Problem 2: Same Ball, Different Zero

The same ball is held $5.0\,\text{m}$ above the ground, but now the release point is chosen as $h=0$.

  1. What is $U_g$ at release?
  2. What is $U_g$ at the ground?
  3. Does the final speed change?
Reveal solution

At release, $U_g=0$. At the ground, $h=-5.0\,\text{m}$, so $U_g=(2)(10)(-5)=-100\,\text{J}$. The final speed does not change because $\Delta U_g$ is still $-100\,\text{J}$.

Level 3 • Solve for Speed

Problem 3: Drop Height to Speed

A cart rolls without friction from rest down a track from a vertical height of $1.25\,\text{m}$. Find its speed at the bottom.

Reveal scaffold

Start with $U_{g,i}+K_i=U_{g,f}+K_f$. Choose bottom as $h=0$.

Reveal solution

$mgh=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Mass cancels. $v=\sqrt{2gh}=\sqrt{2(10)(1.25)}=5.0\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 4 • Work-Energy

Problem 4: Push Across a Floor

A $5.0\,\text{kg}$ box starts from rest. A student does $90\,\text{J}$ of work on the box while friction does $-30\,\text{J}$ of work.

  1. What is the net work?
  2. What is the final kinetic energy?
  3. What is the final speed?
Reveal solution

$W_{net}=90-30=60\,\text{J}$. Since $W_{net}=\Delta K$ and the box starts from rest, $K_f=60\,\text{J}$. Then $60=\frac{1}{2}(5)v^2$, so $v=\sqrt{24}=4.9\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 5 • Ramp with Friction

Problem 5: Lost Mechanical Energy

A $3.0\,\text{kg}$ block slides from rest down a rough ramp with vertical height $2.0\,\text{m}$. Friction transforms $18\,\text{J}$ of mechanical energy into thermal energy. Find the speed at the bottom.

Reveal scaffold

Initial gravitational energy becomes kinetic energy plus thermal/internal energy.

Reveal solution

$mgh=(3)(10)(2)=60\,\text{J}$. Energy left as kinetic: $60-18=42\,\text{J}$. $42=\frac{1}{2}(3)v^2$, so $v^2=28$ and $v=5.3\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 6 • Spring Energy

Problem 6: Spring Launch

A $0.50\,\text{kg}$ cart is pushed against a spring with spring constant $200\,\text{N/m}$. The spring is compressed $0.20\,\text{m}$ and released on a frictionless horizontal track.

  1. What energy is stored initially?
  2. What is the cart's speed after leaving the spring?
Reveal solution

$U_s=\frac{1}{2}kx^2=\frac{1}{2}(200)(0.20)^2=4.0\,\text{J}$. Then $4.0=\frac{1}{2}(0.50)v^2$, so $v=4.0\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 7 • Power

Problem 7: Stair Climb

A $70\,\text{kg}$ student climbs a vertical height of $4.0\,\text{m}$ in $5.0\,\text{s}$.

  1. How much gravitational potential energy does the student gain?
  2. What is the student's average power output?
Reveal solution

$\Delta U_g=mgh=(70)(10)(4)=2800\,\text{J}$. $P=\Delta E/\Delta t=2800/5=560\,\text{W}$.

AP-Style Reasoning Practice

Paragraph Response Prompt

A student claims: “A heavier cart must be moving faster at the bottom of the ramp because it has more gravitational potential energy at the top.”

Write a paragraph that agrees or disagrees with the student. Use energy reasoning.

Reveal strong response

The student is incorrect. A heavier cart does have more gravitational potential energy because $U_g=mgh$, but it also requires more kinetic energy to reach the same speed because $K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$. When energy is conserved, $mgh=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$, and the mass cancels. Therefore, if both carts start from rest at the same height and friction is negligible, they reach the bottom with the same speed.

Graph / Work Prompt

A force varies with position as shown in a graph. The area under the force-position graph is $24\,\text{J}$. The object starts from rest and has mass $3.0\,\text{kg}$.

  1. What does the area represent?
  2. What is the final speed?
Reveal solution

The area under an $F$ vs. $x$ graph represents work. If the net work is $24\,\text{J}$, then $W_{net}=\Delta K=24\,\text{J}$. Since the object starts from rest, $24=\frac{1}{2}(3)v^2$. Thus $v=4.0\,\text{m/s}$.

Fluids Roadmap

1. Density

Density connects mass and volume. It helps predict floating, sinking, and slope meanings in graphs.

2. Pressure

Pressure depends on force per area and depth in a fluid.

3. Buoyancy

Fluids push upward with a force equal to the weight of displaced fluid.

4. Flow

Continuity and Bernoulli connect pipe area, speed, pressure, and height.

AP Exam Mindset: Fluids questions often ask students to compare scenarios. The key is identifying what stays the same, what changes, and whether pressure, force, density, or volume is being compared.

Quick Diagnostic: Common Fluid Traps

Question 1: Pressure at Depth

Two containers hold the same liquid to the same height. One container is wide and one is narrow. How does the pressure at the bottom compare?

Explanation

Gauge pressure in a static fluid is $\rho gh$. Same liquid and same depth means same pressure.

Question 2: Buoyant Force

A floating object is at rest on water. Which statement is true?

Explanation

If the object floats at rest, vertical acceleration is zero, so $F_B=F_g$.

Question 3: Pipe Flow

Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows. What happens to the speed of the water in the narrow section?

Explanation

For incompressible fluid flow, $A_1v_1=A_2v_2$. Smaller area means larger speed.

Pressure in Fluids

Hydrostatic Pressure

Pressure$$P=\frac{F}{A}$$Force spread over area.
Gauge Pressure in a Fluid$$P=\rho gh$$Pressure due to fluid depth.
Absolute Pressure$$P_{abs}=P_0+\rho gh$$Atmospheric plus fluid pressure.
Trap: Fluid pressure at a point does not depend on container shape or total amount of liquid. It depends on depth, density, and $g$.

Pascal's Principle & Hydraulic Systems

Big Idea

Pressure changes applied to an enclosed fluid are transmitted throughout the fluid.

Hydraulic Relationship$$\frac{F_1}{A_1}=\frac{F_2}{A_2}$$Same pressure, different forces if areas differ.
Why hydraulics multiply force: A small force on a small area creates pressure. That same pressure on a larger area produces a larger force.

Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle

Core Idea

Buoyant Force$$F_B=\rho_{fluid}gV_{disp}$$Equal to the weight of displaced fluid.
Floating object: $F_B=F_g$. The object displaces enough fluid so the displaced fluid's weight equals the object's weight.
Trap: A floating object does not necessarily displace its full volume. A fully submerged object does.

Continuity: Flow Rate Stays Constant

Core Idea

Continuity Equation$$A_1v_1=A_2v_2$$For incompressible steady flow.
Student translation: If the pipe narrows, the fluid speeds up so the same volume passes each point each second.

Bernoulli's Principle

Energy in Moving Fluids

Bernoulli Equation$$P+\frac12\rho v^2+\rho gy=\text{constant}$$Pressure energy + kinetic energy + gravitational potential energy per volume.
Common trap: Faster fluid flow generally means lower pressure if height is unchanged.
When to use Bernoulli

Use it for steady, incompressible, nonviscous flow along a streamline. AP problems usually simplify enough for comparison reasoning.

Scaffolded Fluids Problems

Level 1 • Density

Problem 1: Unknown Liquid

A liquid has mass $0.90\,\text{kg}$ and volume $1.0\times10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$.

  1. Find the density.
  2. Would it float on water?
Reveal solution

$\rho=m/V=0.90/(1.0\times10^{-3})=900\,\text{kg/m}^3$. Since this is less than water's density, it would float on water.

Level 2 • Pressure

Problem 2: Pressure at Depth

A diver is $4.0\,\text{m}$ below the surface of water. Use $\rho=1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$ and $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  1. Find gauge pressure.
  2. Explain why container width would not matter.
Reveal solution

$P=\rho gh=(1000)(10)(4)=4.0\times10^4\,\text{Pa}$. Width does not matter because pressure at a depth depends on density, gravity, and depth.

Level 3 • Force from Pressure

Problem 3: Window Underwater

A small underwater window has area $0.30\,\text{m}^2$ and is at a depth where the gauge pressure is $2.0\times10^4\,\text{Pa}$.

  1. Find the force due to the gauge pressure.
Reveal solution

$F=PA=(2.0\times10^4)(0.30)=6.0\times10^3\,\text{N}$.

Level 4 • Floating Object

Problem 4: Floating Block

A $2.0\,\text{kg}$ block floats in water.

  1. What is the buoyant force?
  2. What volume of water is displaced?
Reveal solution

Since the block floats at rest, $F_B=F_g=mg=(2)(10)=20\,\text{N}$. $F_B=\rho gV_{disp}$, so $V_{disp}=20/(1000\cdot10)=0.0020\,\text{m}^3$.

Level 5 • Fully Submerged Object

Problem 5: Apparent Weight

A $5.0\,\text{kg}$ object with volume $2.0\times10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$ is fully submerged in water.

  1. Find the object's weight.
  2. Find the buoyant force.
  3. Find the apparent weight.
Reveal solution

$F_g=mg=(5)(10)=50\,\text{N}$. $F_B=\rho gV=(1000)(10)(2.0\times10^{-3})=20\,\text{N}$. Apparent weight is $50-20=30\,\text{N}$.

Level 6 • Continuity

Problem 6: Narrow Pipe

Water flows through a pipe. At one section, area is $0.060\,\text{m}^2$ and speed is $2.0\,\text{m/s}$. The pipe narrows to $0.020\,\text{m}^2$.

  1. Find the speed in the narrow section.
Reveal solution

$A_1v_1=A_2v_2$. $(0.060)(2.0)=(0.020)v_2$, so $v_2=6.0\,\text{m/s}$.

Level 7 • Bernoulli Reasoning

Problem 7: Horizontal Pipe

Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows. Speed increases in the narrow section.

  1. What happens to pressure?
  2. Justify using Bernoulli's principle.
Reveal solution

Pressure decreases. In a horizontal pipe, $\rho gy$ does not change. If speed increases, the kinetic energy per volume term $\frac12\rho v^2$ increases, so pressure must decrease to keep the Bernoulli sum constant.

Level 8 • Mixed AP Reasoning

Problem 8: Same Depth, Different Area

Two caps are located at the same depth in the same fluid. Cap A has twice the area of Cap B.

  1. Compare the pressure on each cap.
  2. Compare the force on each cap.
Reveal solution

The pressures are equal because depth and fluid density are the same. Since $F=PA$, Cap A experiences twice the force because it has twice the area.

AP-Style Reasoning Practice

Paragraph Prompt: Pressure Misconception

A student says: “The pressure at the bottom of a wide container must be greater because there is more water above it.”

Write a paragraph that agrees or disagrees. Use the pressure-depth relationship.

Reveal strong response

The claim is not correct if the wide container and narrow container hold the same fluid to the same height. Pressure at a depth in a static fluid depends on $P=\rho gh$, so it depends on density, gravity, and depth. The wider container contains more total water and may exert a larger total force on its bottom because the area is larger, but the pressure at the same depth is the same.

Paragraph Prompt: Floating vs. Submerged

A block floats with half its volume underwater. Another identical block is pushed fully underwater and held at rest.

Compare the buoyant force on the two blocks.

Reveal strong response

The fully submerged block experiences a larger buoyant force because buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid. The floating block only displaces fluid equal to half of its volume, while the fully submerged block displaces fluid equal to its entire volume. Therefore, the submerged block displaces more fluid and experiences a greater buoyant force.
